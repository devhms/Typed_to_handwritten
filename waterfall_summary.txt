Summary of Research Paper
Paper Title: A Brief History of the Waterfall Model: Past, Present, and Future
Author: Antonios Saravanos (New York University)
Published: ICCSIT 2025 — International Conference on Computer Science and Information Technology, Paris, France, 2025.

Summary
This paper provides a detailed historical and critical analysis of the Waterfall model, one of the oldest and most widely recognized software development methodologies. The author traces the model from its earliest conceptual roots to its role in modern hybrid development frameworks.
The paper begins by identifying the historical origins of the Waterfall model. The earliest known description of a phase-based software development process was presented by Herbert D. Benington in 1956 at a symposium on digital computers, where he described a structured process for developing SAGE military software. However, the model's formal representation is most often credited to Winston W. Royce's 1970 article published in the proceedings of IEEE WESCON. Interestingly, the paper highlights a common misconception — Royce himself did not praise the model. On the contrary, he described it as flawed and risky, particularly because testing only occurred at the very end of the development cycle. He went on to propose five additional steps to reduce the associated risks, but these refinements were largely ignored by the industry, while his diagram of the "flawed" process became the standard reference. The term "Waterfall" itself was not formally introduced until a 1976 paper by Bell and Thayer.
Despite its criticized rigidity and sequential nature — where each phase such as requirements, design, implementation, testing, and deployment must be completed before the next begins — the Waterfall model gained significant institutional adoption. A key milestone was in 1985 when the United States Department of Defense issued DOD-STD-2167A, which formally mandated the Waterfall approach for defense software development. This cemented its dominance in government, military, and large-scale enterprise projects.
The paper then discusses the model's well-known limitations. Its major weakness is its inability to accommodate changing requirements. Since phases do not overlap and returning to an earlier stage is costly, any change in client requirements mid-project can lead to expensive redesign, redevelopment, and retesting. In dynamic environments, this makes the model particularly unsuitable.
Despite these shortcomings, the author argues that the Waterfall model has not disappeared — rather, it has evolved. The paper examines how the model has been absorbed into modern hybrid methodologies. A review of 24 research papers cited in the study found that all hybrid approaches fundamentally build upon Royce's original Waterfall phases. Three main hybrid patterns are identified: the Waterfall-Agile Approach (WAA), also called "Agilefall"; the Waterfall-Iterative Approach (WIA), called "Waterative"; and the Pipeline Approach (PA). Among these, WAA is the most widely used.
The paper concludes by arguing that the Waterfall model's enduring relevance lies not in its use as a standalone framework but in its adaptability as a structural backbone for modern approaches. It is particularly valuable in contexts where requirements are stable and well-defined, such as in government, aerospace, and mission-critical systems. The author encourages practitioners to make context-aware decisions when selecting a development methodology, recognizing both the strengths and limitations of the Waterfall model.
In summary, this paper effectively bridges history and contemporary practice, demonstrating that the Waterfall model — despite decades of criticism — continues to shape how software is built today, forming the foundation of the hybrid methodologies that dominate modern software engineering.
